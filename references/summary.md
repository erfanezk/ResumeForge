# Writing a Killer Resume Summary

The summary is the first thing recruiters see. It's often the only part they read before deciding to continue or discard the resume.

## Rules

- **2-4 lines** — short and punchy
- **Third person** — "A backend developer with 5 years of experience" not "I am a backend developer"
- **Unique to you** — could NOT be copy-pasted onto another candidate's resume and still make sense
- **Backed by the resume** — every claim in the summary should have evidence in the experience section
- **Tailored to the job** — customize for each application
- **No soft skills** — don't say "team player" or "excellent communicator." Show these through your work experience instead.
- **No personal info** — no age, religion, political views, exact address
- **No hobbies** — unless directly relevant to the role
- **Professional tone** — not too casual, not stuffed with buzzwords
- **Focus on value** — what you bring to the role, not just a list of skills

## What Makes a Summary Good

A good summary answers: "What kind of professional am I, what's my unique value, and what have I actually done to prove it?"

**Bad (generic):** "A passionate software developer with 5 years of experience who loves building great products and is a team player."

This could be anyone. It has no specifics, no proof, and uses clichés.

**Bad (skill list):** "Python, Django, Docker, AWS, PostgreSQL, FastAPI, SQL, Redis, Kubernetes"

This is a skills list, not a summary. It tells me what you know, not who you are.

**Good (specific + backed):** "A self-driven backend developer with 5 years of experience, dedicated to open-source projects with 5 active contributions. Proactively communicates across teams to align technical decisions with business goals. Thrives on solving complex problems with creative thinking and an automation-first approach."

This is specific — "5 active contributions" is verifiable, "proactively communicates" can be checked against work experience entries. It tells a story about who this person is.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too vague** — "Experienced professional with strong skills" says nothing
2. **Buzzword overload** — "Leveraged synergies to ideate scalable solutions"
3. **First person** — "I am a passionate developer..."
4. **Too long** — Should be 2-4 lines, not a paragraph
5. **Just a skill list** — Summary should be about you, not your tech stack
6. **Copy-pasted** — Using the same summary for every application
7. **Soft skills listed directly** — "Excellent communication and leadership skills"
8. **Unprofessional tone** — "Really interesting helping out with open-source stuff. Like for serious, super pumped about it"
9. **Personal info** — "A 22-year-old developer" (irrelevant and can lead to bias)
