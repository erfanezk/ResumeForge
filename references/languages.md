# Languages Deep Dive

## Why It Matters

Language skills give you a competitive edge, especially in today's global job market. Be strategic about how you list them.

## How to List Languages

### Use Standard Proficiency Levels

Stick to recognized levels:
- **Elementary proficiency**
- **Limited working proficiency**
- **Professional working proficiency**
- **Full professional proficiency**
- **Native or bilingual proficiency**

Avoid visual ratings like stars or bars — misleading and not ATS-friendly.

### Relevance is Key

Only include languages relevant to the job or company. If the job doesn't require a specific language and you're not proficient, leave it off.

### Where to Place

Create a dedicated "Languages" section after main sections (Work Experience, Education, Skills). If language proficiency is a key job requirement, also mention it in your summary or work experience.

### Backup Your Claims

If you have certifications validating language proficiency (TOEFL, IELTS), include them — but only if it's a job requirement.

### You're Already Showcasing Writing Skills

Your resume itself demonstrates your English proficiency. Make sure it's well-written and error-free.

## Rules

- ✅ Label skills as: Elementary, Limited Working, Professional Working, Full Professional, or Native/Bilingual proficiency
- ✅ List languages you're proficient in or that relate to the job
- ✅ Backup level with certification hyperlinks if required
- ❌ Don't list languages you're not proficient in (unless native)
- ❌ Don't use stars to express skill level — use proper labels
