# References Deep Dive

## Should You Include References on Your Resume?

In most cases, **no**. Including references directly makes your resume look outdated and wastes valuable space. Prepare a separate references list to provide when requested.

## When to Include References

- **Requested by the Job Posting**: If the description specifically asks for references, include them on a separate page.
- **Entry-Level or Career Change**: If you're new to the job market or switching careers, references might help bridge the gap.

## Creating a Reference List

If you need to provide references:

- **Separate Document**: Create a dedicated "References" page matching your resume's style and formatting
- **Essential Details**: For each reference include: full name, job title, company name, phone number, email, and how you know them (e.g., former manager, colleague)
- **Quality Over Quantity**: 3-5 references ideal. Choose people who can speak positively and knowledgeably about your work.

## Best Practices

- **Ask First**: Always get permission before sharing contact details
- **Keep Them Updated**: Inform references when applying and provide relevant details
- **Tailor Your References**: Choose those most relevant to the target job (e.g., include someone who can speak to leadership skills for a management position)

## Avoid These Mistakes

- **No "References Available Upon Request"**: This phrase is outdated and unnecessary — employers assume you have references
- **Don't Include Personal References**: Stick to professional references unless specifically asked
